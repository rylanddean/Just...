# Just… — Product Proposal

**Version:** 1.0  
**Author:** Ryland Dean

---

## The One-Line Pitch

Just… is a minimal iOS reading app that turns links into a daily reading ritual — and your reflections into a growing personal knowledge base called the Brain.

---

## The Problem

Everyone saves links they never read. Pocket, Instapaper, and reading lists are digital graveyards. Even when people do read, they forget almost immediately — there's no mechanism to capture the thought you had mid-article before it disappears.

Reading apps optimise for saving. Just… optimises for reading and thinking.

---

## The Core Loop

**Save → Strip → Read → Reflect → Brain**

1. **Save** — Add a link to the queue
2. **Strip** — Images removed, minimal reader rendered
3. **Read** — Distraction-free reading view
4. **Reflect** — 60-second writing prompt
5. **Brain** — Stored. Brain grows.

---

## Key Mechanics

### The Queue
An unlimited link queue. No folders, no tags, no organisation. Links flow in order. The reader works through them one at a time.

### The Reader
When a link is opened, content is fetched, images stripped, and a minimal reading experience is presented. Clean typography. No chrome. No distractions.

### The Reflect Window
After each article, a 60-second countdown begins. A blank writing space. No minimum word count. The timer pauses while typing or speaking. Users can capture their thought by typing or via voice-to-text — whichever feels more natural in the moment. Skip is always available but never emphasised.

### The Brain
Every completed article and its reflection is stored in the Brain. The Brain grows with every entry. Its size is the long-term metric — not a streak, but an accumulation. The Brain never shrinks.

### The Streak
Reading at least one link per day maintains the streak. Binary — you showed up or you didn't. No minimum time, no minimum words written.

### Brain Rank
The Brain has a rank that grows with its size. Not a number — a title. Ranks are earned through consistent accumulation. This is the quiet gamification layer.

---

## What Just… Is Not

- **Not a read-later app.** There's no archive. You either read it or remove it from the queue.
- **Not a note-taking app.** Reflections are brief and contextual — not a second brain replacement.
- **Not social.** No sharing, no public Brains, no leaderboards with other users.
- **Not a book reader.** Links only. Focused scope, better product.
- **Not aggressive.** Streak loss is noted quietly. The Brain never shrinks.
- **Not a subscription.** One-time purchase. The Brain is yours.

## Why "Just…"

The ellipsis is the product. That pause between finishing a sentence and knowing what to say next — that's exactly what the reflect window is.

The name works on multiple levels: it belongs to the Just family of apps, it's a literal instruction ("just read"), and the ellipsis signals incompleteness — the thought that hasn't fully formed yet, waiting to be written.

## The Research Case for Reflecting on What You Read

The reflect window in Just… isn't a gimmick. It's grounded in decades of cognitive science research on how memory, comprehension, and critical thinking actually work. This section exists to share that research — both as a rationale for the feature and as content the app itself can surface to help users understand why the 60 seconds matters.

---

### 1. Passive Reading Is Not Enough

Most people re-read content when they want to remember it. Research shows this is one of the least effective learning strategies available. A series of experiments published in *Contemporary Educational Psychology* found that rereading a text produced little to no meaningful improvement in retention or comprehension over a single reading. The effort feels productive, but the memory benefit is minimal.

Simply consuming more content — scrolling more, saving more, opening more tabs — does not compound. Without active engagement, most of what we read is forgotten within hours.

---

### 2. The Testing Effect: Retrieval Beats Re-reading

One of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology is the **testing effect** (also called retrieval practice). When you attempt to recall something from memory — even imperfectly — you strengthen the neural pathways associated with that information far more than re-reading it does.

Research consistently shows that active recall strategies can produce two to three times better long-term retention than passive review methods. A systematic review published on PubMed found that active recall was significantly associated with higher retention and academic self-efficacy. A separate study found that students who used retrieval practice scored an average of 10–12% higher on assessments than those who did not.

The reflect window in Just… is a lightweight form of retrieval practice. Writing or speaking one thought after reading is enough to activate this effect.

---

### 3. Writing Encodes Ideas More Deeply

The act of writing — even briefly — does something passive reading cannot: it forces the brain to reconstruct ideas in your own words, which deepens encoding. Research by Beeson (1996) found that students who wrote after reading outperformed both students who took no notes and students who simply reviewed material without writing.

A meta-analysis by Beesley and Apthorp (2010) found that teaching students effective note-taking strategies was strongly correlated with better achievement outcomes — not because the notes themselves were valuable, but because the act of writing engaged the brain differently than reading alone.

The implication for Just… is significant: even a single sentence of reflection after reading is more cognitively useful than reading a second article.

---

### 4. Voice Has the Same Effect

For users who prefer to speak their reflection rather than type it, the research is reassuring. Studies by MacLeod, Forrin, and colleagues at the University of Waterloo established what they called the **production effect**: producing information — whether by writing, typing, or speaking it aloud — consistently improves memory retention compared to reading silently.

A 2017 study published in the journal *Memory* found that speaking information aloud improved recall, and crucially, that the benefit was strongest when the speech was **self-referential** — when it was your own voice saying your own thought. This is exactly what the Just… voice reflection captures.

A separate study published in *PubMed* confirmed that reading text aloud produces a consistent memory benefit for recall-based questions. The personal, active nature of verbalising a thought is what makes it stick.

---

### 5. Reflection After Reading Improves Comprehension Itself

Post-reading reflection doesn't just help you remember — it actively improves how well you understood what you read in the first place.

Research into **reflection-based questioning** published in *PMC* found that structured reflection after reading had a significant positive effect on reading comprehension scores compared to traditional instruction with no reflection component. Good readers, as identified by Reading Rockets' synthesis of comprehension research, consistently think about and reflect on what they've read after finishing — mentally summarising, connecting ideas, and asking themselves questions about meaning.

Just… formalises this habit into a single 60-second window, lowering the barrier to doing something most skilled readers already do naturally.

---

### 6. Active Engagement Builds Critical Thinking

Beyond memory, active engagement with reading material — as opposed to passive consumption — develops higher-order cognitive skills. Research from BCIT's study skills library found that retrieval practice and self-generated reflection encourage readers to identify gaps in their understanding, make connections between concepts, and apply ideas to new contexts. These are the skills that make reading genuinely useful over time, not just in the moment.

Just… treats every article as a small cognitive workout. The read is the warmup. The reflect is the set.

---

### 7. The Accumulation Effect

Perhaps the most compelling argument for the Brain mechanic is what happens over time. Unlike a streak — which resets — the Brain's entries compound. Each reflection is a small anchor that keeps an idea accessible.

Research on spaced repetition and the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve shows that information not revisited is lost rapidly. The Brain in Just… creates a personal record that works against this forgetting: returning to an old entry reactivates the memory, extending its retention without any additional study.

Over months and years, a Brain with hundreds of entries isn't just a reading log. It's a map of how your thinking has grown.

| Voice reflection equally effective | Production effect | MacLeod & Forrin (2017); PubMed study (2023) |

*The research is clear: reading without reflecting is like exercising without sleeping. The adaptation happens in the pause.*
